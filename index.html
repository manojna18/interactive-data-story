Women fall behind in India's workforce race

Data from the International Labour Organization’s (ILO) labour force survey suggests that a majority of Indian women with advanced education levels continue to stay out of the country’s workforce, especially when married or cohabiting. This is in contrast with married men, who show higher participation levels than their unmarried counterparts.

This is despite the country making notable progress in the last decade in increasing access to education for its girls. Women’s disproportionate participation in unpaid, domestic labour and conservative social norms contribute to keeping them out of paid labour, according to reports from the ILO.

A positive correlation between labour participation of women and their education or skill levels can only be seen in the urban parts of India, according to a 2023 report by the Asian Development Bank Institute. A majority of Indian women who work are from rural areas and pariticipate largely in agriculture, the report suggests.

The gender gap, or the difference in labour participation rates between men and women, is significantly higher in India at 50.9%, the ILO data shows. This is in contrast with countries like Sweden, where the gap is as low as 6.3%.
